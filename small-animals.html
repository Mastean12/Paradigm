Small Animal Care Guide

Housing, diet, socialization, and creating the perfect environment for rabbits, guinea pigs, hamsters, and other small pets.

Welcome to the World of Small Pets!

Sometimes we want a pet in our life but think it's impossible because we are not home enough to take care of it, or we don't have enough room. Consider adopting a small animal such as a hermit crab, mouse, gerbil, hamster, guinea pig, or even a chinchilla!

Depending on which type of animal you adopt, you can provide a comfortable environment using anything from small to large cages or glass aquariums. Our "Pexperts" are here to help you decide which pet is right for you and how to provide them with a happy home.

Housing & Environment Guide

You will need to provide amenities for your new pet, such as bedding or substrate, toys, an exercise wheel, a water dish or drip bottle, a food dish, and grooming equipment.

Cage Systems

Multi-level cages with solid flooring, appropriate bar spacing, and ample floor space.

Bin Cages

DIY enclosures made from plastic storage bins with ventilation added. Great for hamsters and mice.

Aquarium Tanks

Glass aquariums with secure lids. Ideal for small rodents and reptiles that need deep bedding.

Minimum Space Requirements:

  • Hamsters: 450+ square inches of continuous floor space
  • Guinea Pigs: 7.5+ square feet for one, 10.5+ for two
  • Rabbits: 12+ square feet with daily exercise time
  • Mice/Gerbils: 300+ square inches with deep bedding

Social Needs & Companionship

You also need to consider if your new pet will need a friend or if it will be happy living alone. Different species have different social requirements:

Social Animals (Need Companions)

  • Guinea Pigs: Highly social, should never be kept alone
  • Rabbits: Bond strongly with other rabbits
  • Rats: Intelligent and social, thrive in pairs or groups
  • Gerbils: Live in family groups in the wild

Solitary Animals (Prefer to Live Alone)

  • Hamsters: Territorial and will fight if housed together
  • Syrian Hamsters: Must be housed singly after 8 weeks old
  • Some Reptile Species: Many are solitary by nature

Important: Never house different species together. Each species has different needs, diets, and communication methods. Mixed-species housing can lead to stress, injury, or even predation.

Daily Care Routine

  • Fresh food and water
  • Spot clean soiled bedding
  • Social interaction time
  • Exercise/play time
  • Health check (eating, behavior)

Weekly Tasks

  • Full cage cleaning
  • Bedding replacement
  • Toy rotation
  • Weight monitoring
  • Nail trimming (if needed)

Safe Foods

  • Fresh vegetables (limited)
  • Species-specific pellets
  • Timothy hay (herbivores)
  • Occasional fruit treats
  • Fresh herbs (basil, parsley)

Avoid: iceberg lettuce, avocado, chocolate, onions

Proper housing and enrichment are essential for small animal health and happiness.
